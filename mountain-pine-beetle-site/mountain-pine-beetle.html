Task 1

The Problem: Mountain Pine Beetle Outbreaks

Mountain pine beetles (Dendroctonus ponderosae) are small bark beetles native to western North America. They attack and kill pine trees by drilling under the bark, cutting off the flow of water and nutrients, and laying eggs that hatch into larvae that further destroy the tree. While naturally occurring at low levels, rising temperatures have triggered catastrophic outbreaks that are wiping out millions of acres of pine forest.

Task 2

Causes of the Problem

Historical Background

Mountain pine beetles have existed for thousands of years as a natural part of western forests. However, the modern outbreak problem became dramatically more serious in the late 1900s and early 2000s.

Colorado's most recent major outbreak lasted from the late 1990s through 2013, affecting approximately 3.4 million acres of forest — with some areas losing the majority of their pine trees. The problem has grown worse as beetles continue to spread into new forest areas at higher elevations.

Independent variable: temperature  |  Dependent variable: tree death from beetles

Mechanisms: How It Works

Beetles kill trees by drilling under the bark and laying eggs. Larvae hatch and disrupt the tree's vascular system, effectively starving the tree of water and nutrients.

Climate change is the key accelerant: warmer winters allow more beetles to survive cold months, and warmer summers speed up their reproduction cycle. This creates exponentially larger beetle populations each season.

Human role: Burning fossil fuels for transportation, electricity, and industry releases greenhouse gases that raise temperatures. Industries profit from these activities while governments bear the cost of forest management.

Task 3

Who Is Affected?

Human Impacts

  • Dead trees dramatically increase wildfire risk near communities
  • Timber industry loses valuable trees, causing economic damage
  • Governments spend billions removing dead trees and managing wildfires
  • Wildfire smoke causes respiratory illness and poor air quality
  • Falling dead trees create public safety hazards

Ecosystem Impacts

  • Whitebark pine — a K-selected specialist species — suffering major losses in Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem
  • Lodgepole pine heavily affected across Colorado forests
  • Dead trees stop absorbing CO₂, worsening the carbon cycle
  • Forest habitat degraded for wildlife dependent on pine forests
  • Increased dry fuel accelerates wildfire spread

Scientific Evidence & Data

"Temperature increases caused by climate change were a major factor driving the outbreak of mountain pine beetles…across millions of acres in and around Yellowstone National Park in the early 2000s, according to a new study by University of Idaho researchers and their partners."
Why it matters: This quote directly links climate change to the beetle outbreak, showing that warmer temperatures allowed more beetles to survive winter and spread into areas like Yellowstone — the scientific community has confirmed this connection.
Source: USGS — usgs.gov
"In recent years, whitebark pine populations across its range have been compromised by the impacts of mountain pine beetle, blister rust, wildfire, and climate-induced drought."
Why it matters: This shows the beetle is one part of a larger web of threats. Whitebark pine populations are already stressed by multiple factors, making beetle outbreaks even more devastating and urgent to control before ecosystems collapse.
Source: National Park Service — nps.gov
Scientific concern about wildfire risk linked to mountain pine beetles grew dramatically after 2000. The surge in publications and citations confirms this became a major recognized environmental crisis — dead trees create dry fuel that makes wildfires spread faster and burn hotter.
Source: Springer — Landscape Ecology
After the major beetle outbreak in the mid-2000s, forests in affected areas stored significantly less carbon. Dead trees release stored carbon and stop absorbing CO₂ — directly worsening climate change and creating a destructive feedback loop.
Source: Nature, 2008
Task 5

Research & Evaluation of Positive Actions

Addressing the mountain pine beetle crisis requires coordinated action at the individual, government, and engineering levels.

🚗

Individual Actions

Reduce personal carbon emissions by driving less, saving electricity, and using less heating/cooling. Do not move firewood between regions (beetles can hitchhike on wood).

Math: 50 people saving 1 gallon of gas per week = 52,000 lbs of CO₂ avoided per year.

Obstacle: Individual action alone is too small in scale to reverse warming trends. Requires widespread participation and complementary policy.
🏛️

Government / Policy

State and federal forest agencies should create forest monitoring and early removal programs — deploying aerial drones and trained forest workers to identify infected trees before beetles spread to healthy neighboring trees.

Obstacle: Forests are vast, making complete early detection difficult. Some communities oppose tree removal, even when it protects surrounding healthy forest.
🛸

Engineering & Technology

Drone & satellite imaging can detect stressed trees early — before beetles spread uncontrollably.

Verbenone packets (synthetic pheromones) signal to beetles that trees are "full," deterring new attacks without killing beetles directly.

Obstacle: Drone/satellite programs cost money to operate. Verbenone works best when beetle populations are still low — less effective during full outbreaks.
"Verbenone is a synthetic pheromone treatment for high value pine trees that replicates the beetle pheromone, sending a message that the tree is full and that the food supply is insufficient for additional beetles."
Why it matters: Instead of toxic pesticides, verbenone works with beetle biology — confusing them into avoiding trees. This could protect high-value forests, parks, and neighborhoods from further tree loss in a targeted, ecological way.
Source: Forestry Distributing — forestrydistributing.com

Supporting Data & Visuals

Approximately 80% of Colorado's pine forests were affected between 1996 and 2013. Though the outbreak declined, beetle-caused mortality has been rising again since 2020 — reaching 7,600 acres in 2025 (draft). This proves the threat is ongoing and demands continued action.
Source: Colorado State Forest Service / Boulder Reporting Lab, 2026
Areas treated with verbenone pheromone packets had dramatically fewer beetle attacks than untreated areas. At North Shore East, untreated areas saw 50.8% of trees attacked vs. only 21.3% in verbenone buffer zones — proving that early chemical treatment can significantly protect pine trees.
Source: Arboricultural Journal — ISA, 2009

Conclusion

Summary & Next Steps

Mountain pine beetles are a serious and growing environmental crisis. They kill millions of pine trees, increase wildfire risk, damage critical habitats, and disrupt the carbon cycle. Climate change — driven by human fossil fuel use — is the primary reason beetle outbreaks have exploded beyond historical norms. With beetle mortality rising again in Colorado from 2020–2025, urgent and coordinated action is needed.

Expand forest monitoring using aerial drones and satellite imaging
Deploy verbenone treatments early, before outbreaks become severe
Implement early removal programs for infected trees
Reduce fossil fuel emissions to slow climate warming
Protect specialist species like Whitebark and Lodgepole pine
Increase public education on beetle spread via firewood transport
